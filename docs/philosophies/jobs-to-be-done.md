# Jobs to Be Done

> Understand demand by the progress customers are trying to make — the job they hire a product to do — and design around desired outcomes rather than demographic segments or feature requests.

**Discipline:** product · **Origin:** Clayton Christensen, Tony Ulwick · *Competing Against Luck* · (2016)

**Also known as:** JTBD, Outcome-Driven Innovation

## Description

Jobs to Be Done reframes products as services customers hire to make progress in a circumstance. Christensen's popular milkshake example showed that buyers who looked demographically similar could be hiring the product for different jobs, while very different products could compete for the same job. Tony Ulwick's Outcome-Driven Innovation adds a rigorous way to decompose jobs into desired outcomes and measure underserved needs. The philosophy directs product teams away from persona averages and feature wish lists towards the causal forces that create demand: the situation, struggle, desired progress, alternatives and success criteria in the customer's own terms.

**In practice.** Interview recent switchers and non-switchers; reconstruct the timeline and forces behind the purchase; phrase the job and desired outcomes in customer language; map alternatives that perform the same job; and prioritise product changes that improve underserved outcomes rather than merely adding requested features.

## Core tenets

- Customers do not simply buy products; they hire solutions to make progress in a specific circumstance.
- Competition is defined by the job, so non-obvious substitutes may compete more directly than category peers.
- Segment by jobs, struggles and outcomes rather than demographics alone.
- Good discovery seeks causal mechanisms behind switching, not just stated preferences.
- Desired outcomes should guide prioritisation because they reveal where customers are underserved.

## Key ideas

- **Job** — The progress a customer is trying to make in a particular circumstance, including functional, social and emotional dimensions.
- **Forces of progress** — Pushes from the current situation, pulls of the new solution, anxieties and habits that shape whether a customer switches.
- **Desired outcomes** — Measurable criteria customers use to judge successful job execution, often phrased as minimising or increasing a specific result.

## Associated practice patterns

Product / UX patterns that embody or operationalise this philosophy:

- [Jobs-to-be-Done Interview](../product-patterns/jobs-to-be-done-interview.md) — Switch interviews are the primary research mechanism for discovering jobs, alternatives and forces of progress.
- [Jobs-to-be-Done Framing](../product-patterns/jobs-to-be-done-framing.md) — Job statements and outcome framing turn interview evidence into product strategy and prioritisation language.
- [Opportunity Scoring](../product-patterns/opportunity-scoring.md) — Ulwick's ODI prioritises opportunities by outcome importance and satisfaction, closely matching opportunity scoring practice.

## Criticisms / limits

- The phrase is used inconsistently, with competing schools that can make implementation ambiguous.
- Poor JTBD work can reduce rich context to generic job statements that are no more actionable than personas.
- It is strongest for explaining demand and switching, but still needs design, delivery and business-model choices.
