# Hypothesis-Driven Development

> Express product and delivery bets as falsifiable hypotheses, run experiments to test them, and use evidence rather than opinion to decide what to build next.

**Discipline:** product · **Origin:** Barry O'Reilly · *Hypothesis-Driven Development* · 2010s

**Also known as:** HDD, Evidence-Based Development

## Description

Hypothesis-Driven Development brings scientific framing to product work: teams state what they believe will happen, why it matters, how they will test it and what evidence will change their decision. Associated with Barry O'Reilly's lean and evidence-based product practice, it extends Lean Startup thinking into day-to-day feature and capability development. Instead of treating requirements as facts, teams expose assumptions about customer value, usability, feasibility and business impact, then select experiments proportionate to risk.

**In practice.** List assumptions behind a product idea; rank them by risk and uncertainty; write a hypothesis with success and failure thresholds; choose an experiment such as an A/B test, fake door, concierge MVP or prototype; collect data; and explicitly decide whether to continue, change direction or stop.

## Core tenets

- Every significant product bet rests on assumptions that should be made explicit.
- Hypotheses should be falsifiable, measurable and tied to a decision.
- Experiments should be as small as possible while still producing decision-quality evidence.
- Evidence should determine whether to persevere, pivot, scale or stop a solution.
- Learning is only useful when it changes prioritisation, design or investment.

## Key ideas

- **Hypothesis statement** — A clear belief about an intervention, target audience and expected measurable change, written so it can be tested.
- **Assumption mapping** — A way to identify which assumptions are most uncertain and most critical, making them candidates for early experiments.
- **Experiment design** — Choosing the lightest credible test — interview, prototype, fake door, A/B test or release — that can inform the decision at hand.

## Associated practice patterns

Product / UX patterns that embody or operationalise this philosophy:

- [A/B Test Design](../product-patterns/ab-test-design.md) — Controlled experiments are a common way to test hypotheses about product changes against behavioural metrics.
- [Assumption Mapping](../product-patterns/assumption-mapping.md) — Mapping assumptions identifies which beliefs should be tested before further investment.
- [Hypothesis Statement](../product-patterns/hypothesis-statement.md) — Explicit hypothesis statements make product bets testable rather than implicit requirements.

## Criticisms / limits

- Not all valuable product work can be cleanly tested with short-cycle experiments or statistical significance.
- Teams may optimise measurable local hypotheses while missing broader strategic or qualitative value.
- Bad experiment design can create false confidence and worse decisions than explicit judgement would have.
