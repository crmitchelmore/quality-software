# The North Star Framework

> Align product strategy around one guiding metric that captures delivered customer value and business value, then manage the input metrics teams can influence.

**Discipline:** product · **Origin:** Amplitude, John Cutler, Sean Ellis · *North Star Framework* · 2010s

**Also known as:** North Star Metric, NSM Framework

## Description

The North Star Framework gives product organisations a shared measurement model: identify a single metric that best represents the recurring value users receive from the product and that predicts sustainable business success. Amplitude popularised the framework, with lineage through growth practice associated with Sean Ellis and product strategy writing by John Cutler. The North Star is supported by input metrics that teams can influence through product work. Used well, it creates focus across teams without collapsing strategy into a vanity metric.

**In practice.** Define the product's value exchange; select a North Star that captures that value in repeated use; map the inputs that drive it; assign teams to input metrics; instrument dashboards and cohorts; and review both progress and guardrails before changing roadmap priorities.

## Core tenets

- Choose a North Star Metric that reflects customer value delivered, not merely internal activity.
- Connect the metric to business health so improving it should matter commercially.
- Decompose the North Star into controllable input metrics owned by teams.
- Use guardrail metrics to prevent local optimisation that harms quality, trust or long-term retention.
- Revisit the metric as the product, market and strategy evolve.

## Key ideas

- **North Star Metric** — The primary measure of recurring customer value that aligns teams and predicts sustainable business impact.
- **Input metrics** — Leading measures that product teams can influence and that collectively move the North Star.
- **Guardrails** — Metrics that catch harmful side effects such as degraded quality, trust, accessibility, margin or retention.

## Criticisms / limits

- Choosing the wrong metric can focus the organisation on activity rather than real customer value.
- One metric can oversimplify multi-sided marketplaces, enterprise products or portfolios with several value loops.
- Metric focus can crowd out qualitative learning and long-term strategic bets if interpreted too narrowly.
