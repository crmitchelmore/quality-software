# Continuous Discovery

> Make customer learning a weekly team habit, connecting outcomes to opportunities, solutions and experiments so product decisions are continuously informed by evidence.

**Discipline:** product · **Origin:** Teresa Torres · *Continuous Discovery Habits* · (2021)

**Also known as:** Continuous Discovery Habits

## Description

Continuous Discovery, as articulated by Teresa Torres, is a product discovery philosophy for reducing the risk of building the wrong thing. Rather than treating research as a phase before delivery, the product trio — product, design and engineering — continuously engages customers, maps opportunities, compares solution bets and runs small experiments. The Opportunity Solution Tree gives teams a visual structure from desired outcome to customer needs, solution ideas and evidence. The aim is not endless research; it is a steady cadence of decision-quality learning integrated with delivery.

**In practice.** Define an outcome; recruit customers continuously; conduct weekly interviews; capture opportunities as needs, pain points and desires; map them on an Opportunity Solution Tree; compare solutions against assumptions; and run lightweight tests before committing delivery capacity.

## Core tenets

- Discovery should be continuous, not a one-off phase before implementation.
- The product trio should learn together so decisions integrate viability, usability, feasibility and value.
- Start from a desired outcome, then map opportunities before committing to solutions.
- Talk to customers every week and use their stories to uncover needs, pain and context.
- Test assumptions with the smallest useful experiment before scaling investment.

## Key ideas

- **Opportunity Solution Tree** — A visual map connecting an outcome to customer opportunities, possible solutions and experiments that test the assumptions behind them.
- **Product trio** — Product manager, designer and engineer working jointly in discovery rather than handing off decisions.
- **Continuous interviewing** — A regular cadence of customer conversations that keeps evidence fresh and grounded in real contexts.

## Criticisms / limits

- Teams can mistake constant interviewing for progress if insights are not tied to decisions and experiments.
- Recruiting and maintaining weekly customer access is operationally difficult in some markets.
- The method assumes teams have enough autonomy to adjust solutions based on discovery evidence.
