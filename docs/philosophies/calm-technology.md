# Calm Technology

> Design technology that informs without overwhelming, moving between the periphery and centre of attention only when needed.

**Discipline:** ux · **Origin:** Mark Weiser, John Seely Brown, Amber Case · *Designing Calm Technology* · (1995)

**Also known as:** Calm Design

## Description

Calm Technology comes from Weiser and Brown's vision of ubiquitous computing that recedes into the background of everyday life. Rather than demanding constant attention, calm systems make information available at the periphery and move to the centre only when action is useful. Amber Case later translated the idea into practical design principles for connected products and notifications. The philosophy is especially important where software competes for attention: good technology amplifies human capacity without creating noise, anxiety or interruption.

**In practice.** Use quiet defaults, progressive status cues, digestible notifications, skeleton states that reduce anxiety, clear escalation rules and user controls for frequency, urgency and channel. Test whether users feel informed and in control rather than merely more engaged.

## Core tenets

- Require the smallest amount of attention needed for the situation.
- Move smoothly between peripheral awareness and focused interaction.
- Inform without overburdening, interrupting only when the user can or must act.
- Respect human rhythms, social context and environmental limits.
- Make system state legible through subtle, timely cues rather than constant demands.

## Key ideas

- **Centre and periphery** — Information should sit at the edge of attention until it becomes important enough to focus on.
- **Ubiquitous computing** — As computing spreads into everyday environments, interaction design must manage attention rather than maximise engagement.
- **Attention as a scarce resource** — Interruptions have real cognitive and social cost, so notifications and feedback need restraint.

## Associated practice patterns

Product / UX patterns that embody or operationalise this philosophy:

- [Toast Notification](../ux-patterns/toast-notification.md) — Toasts should be reserved for lightweight, non-blocking feedback that does not steal more attention than needed.
- [Skeleton Loading](../ux-patterns/skeleton-loading.md) — Skeleton loading can calmly communicate progress and layout without interruptive spinners or modal waits.
- [System Status Visibility](../ux-patterns/system-status-visibility.md) — Calm systems still make state visible, but in proportion to the user's need to act.

**In tension with**

## Criticisms / limits

- Calmness is context-dependent; an alert that is too subtle can be unsafe in critical systems.
- Business metrics often reward attention capture, making calm design difficult to defend.
- Minimal feedback can become ambiguity if users cannot tell what the system is doing.
