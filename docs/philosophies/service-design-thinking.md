# Service Design Thinking

> Design services as end-to-end, multi-channel experiences by aligning customer journeys with the frontstage and backstage systems that deliver them.

**Discipline:** ux · **Origin:** Marc Stickdorn, Jakob Schneider · *This is Service Design Thinking* · (2011)

**Also known as:** Service Design

## Description

Service design thinking extends UX beyond a single interface to the whole service ecology: people, touchpoints, policies, channels, operations and supporting systems. Stickdorn and Schneider popularised a practical vocabulary for mapping the customer journey and the backstage work that makes it possible. The philosophy is holistic and co-creative: service quality depends on the visible experience and the invisible organisational capabilities being designed together, especially across hand-offs and channels.

**In practice.** Map the customer journey, identify emotional highs and lows, blueprint frontstage and backstage dependencies, invite service staff into design sessions, prototype critical moments, and measure whether hand-offs, recovery and channel transitions improve.

## Core tenets

- Design the service end to end, not isolated screens or touchpoints.
- Understand both the customer's journey and the organisation's backstage processes.
- Co-create with customers, employees and stakeholders who participate in the service.
- Treat services as sequences over time, including before, during and after the main interaction.
- Prototype and test service moments, hand-offs and recovery paths as deliberately as interfaces.

## Key ideas

- **Frontstage and backstage** — Frontstage interactions are visible to customers; backstage processes, tools and roles enable those interactions.
- **Service blueprint** — A map that connects customer actions, touchpoints, employee actions, support processes and evidence across time.
- **Touchpoint orchestration** — Service quality depends on coherent transitions across channels, artefacts and human interactions.

## Associated practice patterns

Product / UX patterns that embody or operationalise this philosophy:

- [Service Blueprint](../ux-patterns/service-blueprint.md) — Blueprinting is the central artefact for aligning customer experience with operational delivery.
- [Customer Journey Map](../ux-patterns/customer-journey-map.md) — Journey maps visualise service experience over time and reveal moments that individual screens miss.
- [Frontstage-Backstage Alignment](../ux-patterns/frontstage-backstage-alignment.md) — Service design depends on coordinating what customers see with the processes that support it.

## Criticisms / limits

- Journey maps and blueprints can become decorative artefacts if operational owners are not involved.
- The broad scope can make service design slow or politically difficult in siloed organisations.
- It may under-specify detailed interaction design unless paired with strong UX execution.
