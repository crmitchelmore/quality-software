# Human-Centred Design

> Design interactive systems by understanding people, contexts and needs, then iterating solutions with user involvement and evidence from use.

**Discipline:** ux · **Origin:** IDEO, ISO 9241-210 · *ISO 9241-210: Human-centred design for interactive systems* · (2010)

**Also known as:** Human-Centered Design, HCD

## Description

Human-centred design makes the people affected by a product the organising reference for design decisions. It starts from an explicit understanding of users, tasks, environments and organisational context, turns that understanding into requirements, and evaluates designs with users throughout an iterative process. In the IDEO and ISO traditions it is not simply being sympathetic to users; it is a disciplined loop of research, synthesis, prototyping and evaluation that keeps human needs, abilities and constraints visible while technical and business constraints are negotiated.

**In practice.** Recruit representative users, observe their work through contextual inquiry, translate findings into needs and design requirements, prototype alternatives, and run usability tests before and after release. Keep research insights connected to product decisions rather than treating them as a one-off discovery phase.

## Core tenets

- Understand users, tasks and environments before committing to a solution.
- Involve users throughout design and development, not only at the end.
- Evaluate designs against observed use and iterate from evidence.
- Address the whole user experience, including accessibility, service context and emotional consequences.
- Work with multidisciplinary teams so human, technical and business constraints are considered together.

## Key ideas

- **Context of use** — A product is judged in the real situation where people use it, including their goals, skills, constraints, devices and environment.
- **Iterative evaluation** — Designs are treated as hypotheses to be tested with representative users and improved across cycles.
- **Human needs before artefacts** — Teams begin with the underlying need or activity, rather than assuming a specific interface or feature is the answer.

## Associated practice patterns

Product / UX patterns that embody or operationalise this philosophy:

- [Usability Testing Session](../ux-patterns/usability-testing-session.md) — Direct observation of representative users performing tasks is the core feedback mechanism for iterative human-centred design.
- [Contextual Inquiry](../ux-patterns/contextual-inquiry.md) — Studying people in their real context grounds design requirements in observed work rather than assumptions.

## Relationships with other philosophies

**Complements:** [The Design of Everyday Things](design-of-everyday-things.md), [Design Thinking](design-thinking.md)

**In tension with**

- [Activity-Centred Design](activity-centered-design.md) — Activity-centred design argues that focusing too narrowly on expressed human needs can miss durable activity structures and system-level workflows.

## Criticisms / limits

- User involvement can become performative if research findings do not affect roadmap and trade-off decisions.
- Focusing on individual users may underweight systemic, organisational or environmental consequences.
- Iteration with users can be slow when teams lack access to representative participants.
