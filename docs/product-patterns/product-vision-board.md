# Product Vision Board

> Capture the product's target customers, needs, value proposition, business goals and product vision on one page so strategic choices stay explicit.

**Discipline:** Product Management · **Category:** product-strategy · **Maturity:** established

**Also known as:** Roman Pichler Product Vision Board

## Description

A Product Vision Board is a compact strategy artefact that links why the product should exist to who it serves, what problems it solves, how it creates value, and which business outcomes make it worthwhile. It is higher-level than a roadmap: the board provides the decision frame that roadmaps, discovery work and release plans should inherit. Done well, it gives teams a shared north star and makes trade-offs visible when attractive features pull in different directions.

**Problem.** Teams accumulate feature ideas, stakeholder requests and market claims without a clear product intent. Different groups optimise for different customers or outcomes, so prioritisation becomes political and the product experience fragments.

**Context.** Use when creating a new product, repositioning an existing one, entering a new market, or aligning a leadership group before detailed discovery and roadmapping.

## Forces

- A vision must inspire alignment while remaining concrete enough to reject off-strategy work.
- Customer, value and business-goal sections need evidence or they become aspirational fiction.
- The board should be stable over quarters, but not so sacred that learning cannot change it.

## Solution

Fill the board collaboratively from evidence: target segments, underserved needs, product value, business benefits and measurable goals. Keep each section brief and testable. Use it as a living strategy contract in roadmap reviews, discovery planning and stakeholder trade-offs.

## When to use

- You need shared product direction before committing to a roadmap or major investment.
- A team receives many feature requests and lacks a coherent decision frame.
- Leadership, product, design and engineering need to agree on which customer and outcome matter most.

## Metrics

Signals that tell you whether this pattern is working:

- Share of roadmap items traceable to a board customer need and business goal.
- Stakeholder alignment score before and after strategy reviews.
- Number of board assumptions replaced by evidence during discovery cycles.

## Examples

### Specific target customer

**❌ Poorer approach**

The customer box says "all small businesses" and the needs box says "manage work better".

**✅ Better approach**

The board names "five-to-fifty person field-service firms whose dispatchers coordinate mobile crews" and the need "reduce missed appointments caused by schedule changes not reaching technicians".

*The poor version is too broad to guide choices. The better version identifies a segment and concrete need that discovery, positioning and roadmap work can test.*

### Using the board to say no

**❌ Poorer approach**

A senior stakeholder asks for an analytics dashboard and the team adds it to the roadmap because it sounds strategic.

**✅ Better approach**

The team checks the board, sees the chosen outcome is reducing dispatch errors for operators, and asks whether the dashboard helps that user in that workflow before considering it.

*A vision board creates value when it changes decisions. If it cannot reject attractive but misaligned ideas, it is only decoration.*

## Anti-patterns

- Treating the board as a marketing slogan rather than a set of strategic choices.
- Filling boxes with broad claims such as "everyone" or "better productivity".
- Creating the board once for a workshop and never using it to reject work.
