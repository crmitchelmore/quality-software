# How Might We Framing

> Reframe an evidence-backed problem into open, optimistic ideation questions that invite multiple solution paths without losing the customer need.

**Discipline:** Product Management · **Category:** problem-framing · **Maturity:** established

**Also known as:** HMW Questions

## Description

How Might We framing converts a problem statement or insight into a question that is broad enough to spark ideas and narrow enough to remain anchored in the customer's situation. The words matter: "How" suggests solutions exist, "Might" leaves room for exploration, and "We" creates shared ownership. Good HMW questions prevent teams from jumping to one solution while also avoiding vague brainstorming prompts.

**Problem.** Ideation sessions either start from a preselected solution or from a problem so broad that the resulting ideas are unfocused and hard to evaluate.

**Context.** Use after discovery synthesis and problem framing, before ideation, design sprints, opportunity mapping or cross-functional workshops.

## Forces

- Too narrow a question smuggles in a solution; too broad a question produces generic ideas.
- Positive framing should not sanitise real constraints or harms.
- Multiple HMWs may be needed for different aspects of one problem.

## Solution

Start from a validated insight or problem statement. Generate several HMW questions at different levels of abstraction, then choose the ones that preserve the customer, situation and desired outcome while leaving many solution approaches open. Use them to guide ideation and later evaluate whether ideas answer the question.

## When to use

- The team has a clear problem and needs divergent solution thinking.
- Workshops are stuck in feature debates and need a neutral framing prompt.
- A design sprint or ideation session needs prompts tied to evidence.

## Metrics

Signals that tell you whether this pattern is working:

- Number and diversity of solution concepts generated per HMW prompt.
- Percentage of selected ideas traceable to an evidence-backed HMW.
- Stakeholder rating of ideation focus and problem alignment after the workshop.

## Examples

### Avoiding solution lock-in

**❌ Poorer approach**

"How might we add automated reminders to reduce missed appointments?"

**✅ Better approach**

"How might we help mobile technicians notice schedule changes before they travel to the wrong job?"

*The poor question preselects reminders. The better question preserves the customer situation and opens space for alerts, routing, confirmations, workflow changes or policy changes.*

### Right level of breadth

**❌ Poorer approach**

"How might we make operations better?"

**✅ Better approach**

"How might we help dispatchers recover quickly when a technician becomes unavailable mid-route?"

*The better prompt is specific enough to generate relevant ideas while not prescribing a feature.*

## Anti-patterns

- Writing "How might we build a dashboard for this?" before exploring alternatives.
- Using HMW questions detached from research evidence.
- Making the question so broad, such as "How might we improve productivity?", that any idea fits.
