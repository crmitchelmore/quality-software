# The Mom Test Interviewing

> Ask about customers' real past behaviour, constraints and commitments rather than pitching your idea and collecting compliments that even your mum would give.

**Discipline:** Product Management · **Category:** customer-discovery · **Maturity:** established

**Also known as:** Mom Test

## Description

The Mom Test is a practical interviewing discipline for avoiding biased customer conversations. The core idea is that bad questions invite people to be nice, speculative or flattering, while good questions force reality into the conversation. Instead of asking whether someone likes an idea, the interviewer asks about the last time the problem occurred, what they did, what it cost, what else they tried and what commitment they will make next.

**Problem.** Teams pitch ideas during discovery, receive encouraging feedback, and mistake politeness or hypothetical interest for evidence that customers will change behaviour or pay.

**Context.** Use in early discovery, founder sales, customer interviews, problem validation and any conversation where the team is emotionally attached to an idea.

## Forces

- People want to be helpful and nice, especially when they know you care about the idea.
- Specific past behaviour is more reliable than future intent, but harder to ask about skilfully.
- The interviewer must resist explaining, defending or selling too early.

## Solution

Keep the idea out of the conversation until you understand the customer's world. Ask about specific recent events, current workarounds, money or time already spent, decision processes and consequences. Listen for concrete facts and commitments. Treat compliments, hypotheticals and vague enthusiasm as weak evidence unless backed by behaviour.

## When to use

- You need honest problem evidence before building or pitching a solution.
- Customer conversations produce praise but little behavioural clarity.
- Founders or teams are too attached to an idea to ask neutral questions.

## Metrics

Signals that tell you whether this pattern is working:

- Percentage of interview notes containing concrete past behaviour, current workaround or existing spend.
- Number of weak compliments explicitly discarded from validation evidence.
- Conversion from interview learning to commitments such as follow-up access, pilot, pre-order or referral.

## Examples

### Bad future question

**❌ Poorer approach**

"Would you use an app that automatically organises your invoices?"

**✅ Better approach**

"Tell me about the last time invoices were disorganised. What happened, how did you fix it, and what did it cost?"

*The poor question invites speculation and politeness. The better question uncovers whether the pain exists and how strongly the customer already acts on it.*

### Compliment versus commitment

**❌ Poorer approach**

A prospect says "That sounds great" and the team marks the idea validated.

**✅ Better approach**

The team asks for a concrete next step: access to sample invoices, an introduction to the finance lead, or agreement to join a pilot when the prototype is ready.

*Compliments are cheap. Commitments reveal whether the problem matters enough for action.*

## Anti-patterns

- Asking "Do you think this is a good idea?" or "Would you pay for this?".
- Treating compliments as validation.
- Talking more than the customer or defending the proposed solution.
