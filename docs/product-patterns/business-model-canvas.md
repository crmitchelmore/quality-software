# Business Model Canvas

> Describe how a product creates, delivers and captures value across customers, channels, revenue, costs, partners and key activities on a single page.

**Discipline:** Product Management · **Category:** product-strategy · **Maturity:** established

**Also known as:** BMC

## Description

The Business Model Canvas frames a product as a system of economic choices rather than a feature set. Its nine blocks — customer segments, value propositions, channels, customer relationships, revenue streams, key resources, key activities, key partners and cost structure — reveal whether product strategy is viable as a business. For product managers, it bridges discovery and commercial reality.

**Problem.** Teams validate that users like a product idea but fail to examine how it will be sold, supported, paid for or scaled economically. Desirability is mistaken for viability.

**Context.** Use for new ventures, new product lines, major pricing or channel shifts, and strategic reviews of an existing product's economics.

## Forces

- Customer value and revenue model must reinforce each other; misaligned pricing can destroy adoption.
- Channels that work early may not scale, while scalable channels may require different packaging.
- Partner and cost assumptions often determine margin or speed.

## Solution

Fill each block with current evidence and explicit assumptions. Identify the riskiest blocks — often channel, willingness to pay or cost-to-serve — and turn them into experiments. Revisit the canvas as pricing, packaging and operations mature, keeping business-model changes connected to product decisions.

## When to use

- A product idea needs commercial validation, not just user desirability validation.
- Entering a new segment or channel may change cost, revenue or partner assumptions.
- Leadership needs a concise view of how strategy becomes a sustainable business.

## Metrics

Signals that tell you whether this pattern is working:

- Gross margin or contribution margin by customer segment.
- Customer acquisition cost, payback period and lifetime value assumptions tested against reality.
- Percentage of high-risk business-model assumptions with active experiments or evidence.

## Examples

### Viability beyond desirability

**❌ Poorer approach**

Interviews show users love automated reporting, so the team concludes the product is viable.

**✅ Better approach**

The canvas also tests whether buyers will pay enough, whether a sales-assisted channel is required and whether report customisation makes cost-to-serve too high.

*User enthusiasm is necessary but not sufficient. The better approach examines value capture and delivery.*

### Channel-product fit

**❌ Poorer approach**

A self-serve SMB product is repackaged for enterprise without changing onboarding, procurement or support.

**✅ Better approach**

The team updates the canvas to reflect enterprise buyers, procurement channels, implementation partners and higher support costs before committing to the segment.

*A segment shift is a business-model shift. The canvas makes hidden operational and economic changes visible.*

## Anti-patterns

- Treating the canvas as a pitch-deck graphic instead of a map of testable assumptions.
- Optimising for users while ignoring buyer, channel and support economics.
- Assuming an existing company business model automatically fits a new product.
