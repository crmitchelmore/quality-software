# Jobs-to-be-Done Framing

> Frame opportunities around the progress customers seek in a situation, including functional, emotional and social dimensions, rather than around features or demographics.

**Discipline:** Product Management · **Category:** problem-framing · **Maturity:** established

**Also known as:** JTBD Framing

## Description

Jobs-to-be-Done Framing translates discovery evidence into job statements and forces of progress. It defines the situation in which a customer struggles, the progress they seek, and the outcomes they use to judge success. The frame helps teams see competitors, workarounds and product opportunities through the customer's goal rather than through the company's category or feature taxonomy.

**Problem.** Product opportunities are framed as features, personas or market segments, obscuring the reason customers change behaviour and the alternatives they actually consider.

**Context.** Use after JTBD interviews, during proposition design, opportunity mapping, onboarding work or churn analysis.

## Forces

- A job must be stable enough to guide strategy but specific enough to inform product choices.
- Functional outcomes are visible, while social and emotional outcomes are often decisive but under-stated.
- Different actors in B2B may have different jobs within one purchase.

## Solution

Synthesise interview evidence into job frames such as "When [situation], I want to [progress], so I can [outcome]". Add forces pushing toward change, habits pulling back, anxieties, alternatives and success criteria. Use the frame to evaluate solutions, messaging and onboarding against the progress customers are trying to make.

## When to use

- Customer research reveals repeated struggles but the team lacks a clear opportunity frame.
- The product is compared with unexpected alternatives or workarounds.
- Segmentation by role or company size does not explain adoption behaviour.

## Metrics

Signals that tell you whether this pattern is working:

- Activation or conversion lift for flows redesigned around a validated job.
- Percentage of top opportunities expressed as job frames with supporting evidence.
- Reduction in roadmap items framed primarily as feature requests.

## Examples

### Progress over feature

**❌ Poorer approach**

"When I use the product, I want reports, so I can see reports."

**✅ Better approach**

"When month-end closes, I want to explain revenue anomalies quickly, so I can answer finance questions before leadership review."

*The better frame describes a situation, progress and outcome. It leaves room for multiple solutions beyond reports.*

### Alternatives from the job

**❌ Poorer approach**

The team analyses only direct reporting-tool competitors.

**✅ Better approach**

The job frame reveals the customer currently uses analyst Slack threads and spreadsheet checks, so those alternatives shape product requirements.

*JTBD defines competition by the progress sought, not by software category.*

## Anti-patterns

- Writing jobs as features: "When I log in, I want a dashboard".
- Treating JTBD as a slogan and skipping evidence from real customer stories.
- Ignoring buyer, user and approver jobs in complex purchases.
